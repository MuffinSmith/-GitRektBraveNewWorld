  Brave New World, written by Aldous Huxley, speaks of several different themes. Hierarchy for one is perhaps the most prominent of the majority of those themes, as even from the beginning of the chapter, segregation by rank, as well as discrimination by rank was upheld by the Controllers. However, another theme seen in the beginning is none other than pregnancy substitution. As spoken within the book, "Dr. Wells told me that brunettes with wide pelvises, like me, ought to have their first Pregnancy Substitute at seventeen" (Huxley 44). Now, according to this world, a pregnancy substitute is an intravenous injection that tricks the body into thinking it is pregnant and that balances the hormones. Comparing such a bizarre process to real life does indeed seem rather peculiar in every sense of the word. However, rather than looking at it literally, one must see it from a different perspective. Specifically, the only thing to compare these 'pregnancy substitutes' to is a little concept known as 'synthetic happiness'. The concept of 'synthetic happiness' involves the creation of one's own happiness when the original desire is not fulfilled. In other words, it is best known as 'sugar-coating'. In the words of the speaker who all but advertises the use of synthetic happiness, "We synthesize happiness, but we think happiness is a thing to be found" (Gilbert 3). In other words, we tell ourselves that we are happy with the results of what we have achieved or received until our mind comes to believe that we truly are happy with the results. This sounds rather similar to the pregnancy substitutes found within the book, which, by nearly the same process, tricks the body into believing that it is pregnant, as spoken before. These two are one of many bridges connecting the book's ideals with real life society.
